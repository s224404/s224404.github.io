The Story of the Streets: Drug related crime in San Francisco

Investigating San Francisco's Drug and Narcotics Data (2003—2025)
Data from SF crime data containing all reported crime incidents of different types with various variables such as location, time, and district.

Historical Peak

The data shows a clear peak in drug and narcotic arrests in the year 2008, followed by a steady decline in the years after. This may be due to a shift toward treatment and harm reduction rather than punishment, along with declining trends in drugs like heroin and cocaine and thus fewer overall arrests.

The Pandemic Silence

The sharp drop during the COVID-19 pandemic is likely due to lockdowns and reduced social activity, which led to fewer police interactions and arrests. At the same time, law enforcement priorities shifted, meaning fewer drug-related offenses were recorded, even though drug use may not have decreased.

Post-Pandemic Boom

After the pandemic, drug and narcotic offenses begin to rise steadily again. This increase may reflect a return to normal social activity, more active policing, and the growing impact fentanyl boom all over the US.

Mapping the Crimes

Beyond the timeline, the crisis is not evenly distributed across the city. By looking at the ratio of drug-related incidents, we can see how certain police districts carry a significantly higher burden than others. While San Francisco is a mosaic of neighborhoods, the data highlights a distinct geographic concentration.

The Epicenter: Tenderloin

Zooming in, the Tenderloin district stands out as the historical and current epicenter. The yellow glow on the map isn't just data — it represents the intense concentration of open-air drug markets and enforcement. This district consistently reports a ratio far beyond any other area, making it the focal point of SF's drug policy debate.

The Crime Fingerprint

Every district has a unique "crime DNA." Historically, Larceny Theft is the dominant category across San Francisco. But look at the shapes — some districts are more "balanced" than others.

The impact of drug crimes in Tenderloin

When comparing the Tenderloin to the Mission, the difference is striking. While Larceny Theft is 7 times more common than drug crimes city-wide, in Tenderloin, they are almost neck-and-neck. This DNA has remained consistent for over two decades.

To Conclude

From the peak of 2008 to the post-pandemic increase, San Francisco's drug landscape is constantly shifting. While the city moves toward new models of care and enforcement, the data tells a story of deep geographic concentration. The streets of the Tenderloin district remain the epicenter of a crisis that is as much about location as it is about law.
